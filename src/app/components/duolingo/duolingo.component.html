The Problem

Users are not learning a second language through apps like Duolingo because they do not dedicate enough time to their studies.

Solution

Make learning fun. Duolingo has done a pretty good job at this. The reminders and extra circular activates now also have to be fun. Polyglot Alex Redfern stated in his interview that learning a language takes motivation, time, and effort. The user has to enjoy the process to succeed at these three key ingredients.

Forming a Hypothesis

These past few years I have been married to a Latino and his parents only speak Spanish. I was born and raised in California where I took extra English classes and did not have to take any Spanish classes until I was in college, where I never spoke Spanish. I spent hours making flash cards and taking tests where I practically just reiterated back to the professor what I had memorized or even worse, could only recognize. Before digging super deeply into my problem, I know it is important to state a problem hypothesis and then test it. Testing the hypothesis lets me develop a stronger understanding for the problem, discover what other users have experienced through Duolingo, see what they would recommend, and then identify if this is a problem worth solving.

My Hypothesis

I believe Duolingo users would be more dedicated to learning the language that they want to learn if the learning did not feel like a daily choir, more like an activity that they look forward to doing such as reading, writing, texting, listening, and talking—but in the way that user thinks is fun.

Gathering Information

User Research

While I develop my hypothesis, I ask myself questions like, who are the people who use Duolingo, what kind of experience are they having, are they able to be dedicated to the lessons, what type of feedback will I need to get to see if my hypothesis is valid?

User Interviews

I had a total of three Duolingo users in person to complete the face to face interviews. I asked questions like, “How often do you think to use Duolingo?” “Do you go days without using it, why?” “What would you add or change if you could on Duolingo?” “Have you thought of changing programs to see if they are better; have you changed?” “Was it better and why?”

Online Research

Fluent posted an article stating lots of reasons to like Duolingo, but they also said why they did not enjoy it. Their main reason was, “It’s the Vocab”. They do not like the sentences that the app is teaching. They find them to be useless sentences. Lorne commented on the article saying she used it for six months before her trip to Brazil and was able to talk to taxi drivers, ask for directions, and hold simple conversations. Andras Toth comments that Duolingo is good for beginners, learning the basic language, but the app should obviously be supplemented with extra resources. Another article by Brent Van Arsdell states that if you do Duolingo for five minutes a day, that is the amount you will forget a day. He suggests doing it for three to four hours a day. The Linguist says that he got very frustrated because he is not a beginner and he was stuck learning random simple sentences when he wanted to learn sentences he could use. He says that it is too time consuming to get to the lessons he needs/wants.

Key insights:

The users forget about the app. They do want to learn the language, but they do not want to put the effort into it. Some of the users have changed and found that they thought the other app or system was working better, but they concluded that it was working better because it was new and they were excited to use it so they used it more often and sometimes longer. Duolingo needs to be used for a lot more than five minutes a day. The content is not what the user would use in the real world (‘the bird is here’ opposed to learning the sentence, ‘how was your day today?’) Users don’t want to be stuck on the beginner levels if they aren’t a beginner.

The Opportunity

The findings of my research lead me to the conclusion that hundreds of users are trying to learn a language through Duolingo, however; they are frustrated that they are not learning the language to a fluency that can be used in the languages native land. If we solve the problem that users are not dedicating enough time to their studies, customers satisfaction will increase.

Design Goals

Make learning not feel like learning Help customers understand what they like to do to learn a language such as watching t.v. or reading a book in that language. Help users understand they need outside sources The slogan is “Five minutes a day”, what is should be is, “Five minutes a day, one episode of (TV show), and a chapter in a recommended book”. Make learning feel worth their time Teach the students on the level that they are and teach them phrases and words that improve their vocabulary in a way they can use in the real world.

Persona Development

Creating personas is important to me so that I can remember the who the design is aimed towards. These personas help me refer back to my original thoughts and feelings I had when I was interviewing and researching Duolingo users.

User Journey Map

By laying out the journey map that users go on, I am able to see multiple pain points instead of the few that I have encountered myself. I like to also find potential pain points at this point and see if I can catch them and add them to the design process now instead of later.

Exploring Ideas

After spending a fair amount of time getting to know the users, the lovers, and the haters, I wrote down all the ideas that I could think of. I wanted to broaden the horizon on the possibilities to fix the problem that users are not learning a second language through apps like Duolingo because they do not dedicate enough time to their studies.

How Might We Question:

How might Duolingo users wanting to learn a new language ensure they spend enough time to learn it?
The umbrella answer is, to create ways that are fun to the user so they spend more time using the language they are trying to learn. Ideas on how to create the include:

Idea 1

Make the reminder popup with a word that the user can learn. Have a popup a few times a day to teach the user a word every time. These words go into a word bank that can be clicked on in Duolingo to be reviewed when wanted.
Pros
If the user glances at it they will see the word and if they give it a minute they can read the definition and the sentence it is used in (in the foreign language). Maybe some of the words will stick and it won’t take a long time to read through.
Cons
The user might not read it at all before they clear it out.

Idea 2

Take a fluency test to determine what level the user is on. Can be retaken at any time. Let users decide what level they are on.
Pros
This would help users to enter in the level that would be most helpful for them making the lessons more time worthy and the words and phrases more useful to were the user is at in the learning process. If you can take it anytime, if you mess up or are accidentally a super star the first time, you can take it again and see what happens.
Cons
The user might test too high and then the lessons will be too hard or test too low and it will be how it is now.

Idea 3

Recommend books, podcasts, TV shows, and music the user should read/listen to.
Pros
This will guide users to outside sources that are in the user’s level so it will be more fun because the user will understand it therefore want to use it.
Cons
How would you do this without having it take over the app (in the design process) and how will the users get the books or shows etc.?

Idea 4

Give the user a texting buddy. If the user doesn’t want a real person, set up so user can text with the owl from Duolingo. (Premade discussions—lots of them). Split the screen so you can see the translation of the texts.
Pros
Users will have someone (or the robot) to text so that they can practice what they learn. The split screen (for translation) will help users see where they are messing up in their texts giving them a chance to fix it right then and learn from their mistakes. Hopefully this could learn to phone conversations or meetups (as long as it is safe).
Cons
The screen might be too cluttered. What if the user’s texting buddy never responds?

Idea 5

MORE talking. People say they took years of a language in high school and college and can’t speak a word of it. Make the users talk talk talk. Have whole speaking lessons, where all the user does is talk and repeat sentences. Have a level where users respond to the phrase being said instead of repeating it.
Pros
They say the best way to learn a language is to speak it. If the app is making you speak, you have something to speak. If you learn phrases it can lead to conversations.
Cons
Some people are not comfortable speaking new languages. You can’t always speak in public.

Ideas to Move Forward With

Make the reminder popup with a word that the user can learn. Recommend books, podcasts, TV shows, and music the user should read/listen to. Give the user a texting buddy. Have a level where users respond to the phrase being said instead of repeating it.

Why I did NOT Choose Idea Two

Idea two already exist in Duolingo. Users have to pay more to do it.

Why did I Move Forward with These Four Ideas?

These ideas demonstrate how to reach the main values that are proposed for the next Duolingo enhancement.
  • Valuable Knowledge – with the ability to speak more, learn from sources that native speakers are using, and practice responding, users will be able to take the knowledge they learn here into the street.
  • Memorable – with the option of spending more time learning more words, sentences, and phrases, more of the lesson learned that day will be remembered for the next time the user comes back.
  • Product Differentiation – by having outside sources and an option to respond to the phrases, users will have a unique experience that will prepare them for conversations in the desired language.

User Flow Diagram

I like to get my user flow diagram mapped out here. It helps me to start thinking about the process that the user will go through to achieve the desired tasks. I try to think how the steps would look like to the user. I also try to identify if I am going to have to change any of the original screens and how.

Old and New Screens

In this case, I will need to change the reminder screen to the new design and the homepage screen to include a new icon. I don’t want to change the feel it already has so I am using a lightbulb that can be found in other parts of the lesson to add the new features. I also will create a new Duo for my new speaking class.

Things to Consider

I do this step in order to think of small details that I haven’t realized might be a problem yet. Here are a few of the considerations I came up with:
  • Where will the outside sources come from?
    • Will we tell users where to find them?
  • Will there be enough people signing up for a text buddy to have a text buddy?
    • What happens if someone wants a text buddy but there is no one else signing up for a text buddy?
      • What if these two people decide to meet up and someone gets hurt, is that on me?
  • What if the robot is boring to text?
    • How would we make that conversation fun and how would the robot know what to respond?
The speaking lesson, we will need to have multiple answers that are accepted because it would be no fun for the user to have to read the robots mind to get it right.

Answers

The outside sources can be from anywhere. People can look up books at their libraries, find music on whatever app they have chosen, and look up podcasts on whatever outlet they use. So, we kind of tell them by saying, “Here is a book recommended for you, look for it in your library.” If not enough people sign up to be text buddies, then we can just have the robot. If people decide to meet, that is on them (just like if they meet each other on other apps). Duolingo can use the same programs that the help robots found in the bottom of company pages uses. They seem to have multiple answers and are fairly on topic. Let the user choose the response, but that could be boring and not fun for the user.

New Things to Consider

What if they don’t have a library by them? Can they find the books online? Should there be an amount of time between receiving a text and sending a text so the other person doesn’t have to keep waiting when they really want to practice? How will we show the correct answers? (Give the phrase that needs to be in the sentence, show examples.

Sketching and Wireframing

Once I researched and explored solutions to the ideas, I wanted to test these ideas with Duolingo users. I did some qualitative testing with my users on a few screens. I used Sketch to create my prototypes and mock up my screens. I conducted usability test with four users.

Research Findings:

  • Users like the idea of being able to text a buddy. They however did not like the way the screen was presented and expressed that they would not use that feature if it looked like it did. It was too hard to read.
  • Users want to know if they will be able to try the speaking sections they get wrong again right away.

Usability Issue:

Users did not like that only two words would fit on a line when trying to text and find the translation.

Design Decision:

Make the translation pop up behind the text after choosing the translation button. It will look like the user is texting themselves to make it not confusing that the text behind the edit field is the translation of the edit field.

Feedback

After making the design change to create a better feeling for the user, I presented my prototypes to two users. I received the following responses:
  • “Wow, these are such good ideas. They fit right into Duolingo’s style. Duolingo should hire you!” -Tami

Revisit the Persona Problem

Now when the user gets notifications from Duolingo, he reads the daily phrase—even if he stops there he still earned something. However, he opens the app and does a few lessons. Later on in the day Kevin (his texting buddy) sends him a message. Our user opens his message and spends a few minutes typing out the text he wants. Even though he does not spend a lot of time doing the lessons in Duolingo, he is learning more and more every day little by little. Our user is beating the problem, he is learning a language through a phone app like Duolingo.

What Went Well?

Having a vague knowledge of what language learners are hoping to achieve and in what time frame. Research, learning more about what learners actually have to do to learn the language. Ideas to get the users accomplishing those goals.

What Was Hard and Could Have Gone Better?

Making the ideas look acceptable on a phone screen. Coming up with icons and symbols that go within the Duolingo style guide. Getting users to have negative criticism to help my design and ideas have a better flow to them.

What I Learned

I could have studied Duolingo’s app longer before diving in. I did look up their style guide, but it is old and does not fit the app anymore. I could have learned more from getting opinions from users of different language apps. It could have helped me to come up with solutions or ideas that other apps are trying to use. Of course, the more people that test it, the better!
